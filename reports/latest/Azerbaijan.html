1 Daily New Cases

According to the Johns Hopkins University global COVID-19 data set [1] a total of 1645 COVID-19 cases were reported in Azerbaijan on 2020-04-26. This number of cases increased by 1.5 percent over the cumulative total cases known one day earlier. At this rate of cases double every 76 days and the basic reproduction rate \(R_0\) in Azerbaijan can be estimated at 0.9, an estimate of the average number of other people infected by a single infected person. 1.1 Double sigmoidal model for daily new cases

Daily new cases peek after a certain time of growth that follows a sigmoidal function and then recedes to a lower level as the epidemic wave fades out and enters an endemic state. This behavior can be modelled with a double-sigmoid function \(f_{base}\), that is obtained by the product of two sigmoidal functions [2].

\[ f_{base}(t) = \frac{1}{ 1+e^{-\alpha_{i} * (t - t_{i})} } * \frac{1}{ 1+e^{-\alpha_{d} * (t - t_{d})} }\]

This function has a maximum \(f_{max} = max(f_{base}(t_{max}))\) at \(t_{max}\).

The model can obtain predictions related to an observed maximum of daily new cases \(I_{max_{d}}\) and an endemic number of daily cases \(I_{final_{d}}\) as an asymptote (that is ideally 0). Let \(f_{d}(t)\) be a piecewise function that switches between the growth and decay phase at \(t_{max}\), such that

\[\begin{aligned}f_{d}(t) = \left\{ \begin{array}{cc} \frac{I_{max_{d}}}{f_{max}} * f_{base}(t) & \hspace{5mm} t \leq t_{max} \\ \frac{I_{max_{d}} - I_{final_{d} }}{f_{max}} * f_{base}(t) + I_{final_{d}} & \hspace{5mm} t > t_{max} \\ \end{array} \right. \end{aligned}\]

where \(t_{i}\) is the midpoint and \(\alpha_{i}\) the slope of the increase of cases and \(t_{d}\) is the midpoint and \(\alpha_{d}\) is the slope of the decrease in cases. \(I_{max_{d}}\) the peak of new cases and \(I_{final_{d}}\) the asymptotic final value of new cases at the end of the infections wave. Figure 1.2 shows the current double-sigmoidal model that is fitted for the case time series of Azerbaijan.

2 Cumulative cases

The cumulative total of COVID-19 cases in Azerbaijan is the number of all COVID-19 cases known up to yesterday and includes all patients already recovered from the infection. This corresponds to the Infected (I) number in the standard epidemiological SIR model. 2.1 Gompertz model for cumulative cases

The Gompertz model [3] is a sigmoid model that is often fitted to growth data, and often prefered over the standard logistic model (als called Verhulst model) due its asymetry. The Gompertz can be considered as a special case of the four parameter Richards model and is computationally easier to fit as it has one parameter less. The model is based on the cumulative distribution function \(f_{c}(t)\), which is a three parameter logistic function:

\[ f_{c}(t) = I_{max_{c}} * e^{ - \beta_{c} * e^{ - \alpha_{c} * t }} \]

Where the parameter \(I_{max_{c}}\) is the estimated maximum number of cumulative cases (asymptote), \(\beta_{c}\) inflects the curve along time, and \(\alpha_{c}\) is the maximum absolute growth rate of cumulated cases.

This function is the integral of the probability density function (PDF) developed by Benjamin Gompertz in 1825 to model human mortality, hence his probability densitiy function (PDF) is the derivative of the CDF:

\[\frac{\partial f_{c}(t)}{\partial t} = I_{max_{c}} * \beta_{c} * \alpha_{c} * e^{ - \beta_{c} * e^{ - \alpha_{c} * t } - \alpha_{c} * t} \]

3 Projections

3.1 Methodology

Putting in future dates into the current model allows to project future cases. Note: The actual development can differ from model predictions, for example if social distancing norms change or methods in data collection are altered. If such changes happen, a model fitted to the past is no longer valid.

4 Discussion and outlook

Epidemiological models are useful for policy makers, e.g. to assess (lack of) ICU beds, required (additional) restrictions of daily life, etc. However models require (often disputed) assumptions for model parameters. The data-driven approach demonstrated here avoids parameter assumptions and uses past data to fit models that not only describe the past developments but provide reliable forecasts for the nearer future of an epidemic. Next to policy makers, the general public can use the projections to assess their expectations when business or schools might reopen (or reclose).

As a next step the convergence of models could be investigated more closely to assess whether model divergence is an early indicator for a general change in infective behavior. Additional data about the population and population structure could be used to compare between countries, for example to assess the effectiveness of different public policies for controlling the disease.
